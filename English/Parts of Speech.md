### **1. Noun**

A **noun** is a word that names a person, place, thing, or idea.

- **Test**: ==Can you put an article like **_a, an,_ or _the_** before the word?==
- **Example**: _Justice_, _teacher_, _happiness_.
    - Sentence: _The justice was fair._ (Justice = noun)

### **2. Verb**

A **verb** is an action word or a state of being.

- **Test**: Can the word express something being done or a state?
- **Example**: _Run_, _think_, _is_.
    - Sentence: _She runs daily._ (Run = verb)

### **3. Adjective**

An **adjective** describes a noun (it answers _which, what kind of, how many_).

- **Test**: ==Does the word modify or describe a noun?==
- **Example**: _Beautiful_, _red_, _proportionate_.
    - Sentence: _She has a proportionate figure._ (Proportionate = adjective)

### **4. Adverb**

An **adverb** describes a verb, an adjective, or another adverb (it answers _how, when, where, to what extent_).

- **Test**: ==Does the word modify an action, adjective, or another adverb?==
- **Example**: _Quickly_, _very_, _yesterday_.
    - Sentence: _She spoke proportionately louder._ (Proportionately = adverb)

---

### **How to Identify in Cases like "==Proportionate=="**

The word **"proportionate"** is usually an **adjective**, meaning "balanced or appropriate."

- **Sentence**: _They received proportionate rewards for their efforts._ (Describes "rewards" = adjective)

But **"proportionately"** (with -ly) would become an **adverb**, describing "how" something is done:

- **Sentence**: _The benefits were proportionately divided._ (Describes "divided" = adverb)

---

#### **General Tips to Identify Parts of Speech**

1. **Check the sentence context**: The role of a word depends on its function in the sentence.
    
    - _He is a **runner**._ (Noun: A person who runs)
    - _She can **run** fast._ (Verb: Action)
2. **Suffix Clues**:
    
    - **Noun suffixes**: _-ment, -ness, -tion, -ity_ (_Happiness, proportion_)
    - **Adjective suffixes**: _-ful, -less, -able, -ate_ (_Beautiful, proportionate_)
    - **Adverb suffixes**: _-ly, -ward_ (_Quickly, proportionately_)
    - **Verb suffixes**: _-ize, -ify_ (_Realize, classify_)
3. **Modify the sentence**: Try replacing the word with others of the same part of speech to see if it fits.
    
4. **Look at the word's position**:
    
    - Before a noun? Likely an adjective. (_A proportionate response_)
    - After "is/are"? Likely a noun or adjective. (_The answer is proportionate._)
    - Modifying a verb? Likely an adverb. (_He acted proportionately._)

To determine whether **"swimming"** is a participle, gerund, verbal noun, or infinitive, let’s break it down step by step.

### **1. Understanding the Sentence**

- **"A swimming snake bit him in the leg."**
- The word **"swimming"** is describing the noun **"snake."** It tells us _what kind of snake_ it is.
- Words ending in **-ing** that act as adjectives are **participles.**

---

### **Answer**:

**"Swimming" is a participle** because it is used as an adjective modifying the noun "snake."

---

### **How to Identify the Forms (Gerund, Participle, Verbal Noun, Infinitive)**

| Participle | adjective      |
| ---------- | -------------- |
| Gerund     | Noun           |
| Infinitive | to + base_form |



---

1. ==**Gerund**==
    - **Definition**: ==Acts as a **noun**==, usually a subject or object in a sentence.
    - **Example**: _Swimming is a good exercise._
        - "Swimming" is the subject of the sentence → gerund.
    - **Clue**: If the -ing word acts as a **thing** (noun), it’s a gerund.

---

2. ==**Verbal Noun**==
    - **Definition**: ==A **noun**== form derived from a verb but ==does **not** act like a verb==.
    - **Example**: _The swimming of the athletes impressed the coach._
        - "The swimming" refers to the act itself (noun), often used with an article (the, a).
    - **Clue**: Verbal nouns are purely nouns and don't function as verbs.

---

3. ==**Infinitive**==
    - **Definition**: The ==base form of a verb==, often preceded by "to."
    - **Example**: _I love to swim._
        - "==To swim==" is the infinitive.
    - **Clue**: If the verb form starts with **"to"** and doesn't modify a noun, it’s an infinitive.

4. **Participle**
	🔹 **Definition:** A **verb** form that **acts as an ==adjective**== to describe a noun.  
	🔹 **Types:**
	- **Present Participle (-ing form)** → _smiling, running, dancing_
	- **Past Participle (-ed, -en form)** → _bored, broken, written_  
	    🔹 **Example Sentences:**
		    The **running** water is cold. (_Running describes water → Present participle_)

### **Common Steps to Identify**

5. Look at the word’s role in the sentence:
    
    - **Describes a noun** → Participle.
    - **Acts as a noun** → Gerund or Verbal Noun.
    - **Base form of a verb** → Infinitive.
6. Ask yourself:
    
    - Is it describing something? (Participle)
    - Is it acting as a subject/object? (Gerund)
    - Does it have an article like "the"? (Verbal Noun)
    - Does it involve "to"? (Infinitive)

## eg.
`The ____ received a book as a reward for their hard work.` 
	1. students each have 
	2. students each has 
	3. student each has to 
	4. students each was to

In this sentence, the correct option is:

**"students each have."**

---

### **Rule for Using "Each" with "Has" or "Have"**  #lesion

20. **Subject Determines the Verb**:
    - When **"each"** comes **after the plural noun**, the verb agrees with the plural noun.
        
        - Example: _The students each **have** a book._
        - Here, "students" (plural) is the subject, so "have" is used.
    - When **"each"** comes **before the noun**, the verb agrees with "each," which is singular.
        
        - Example: _Each student **has** a book._
        - Here, "each" is the subject, so "has" is used.

---

### **Explanation of the Sentence**

- The plural subject is **"students"**.
- The word **"each"** is used to indicate that every student individually received a book, but the verb must agree with the plural subject **"students"**.
- Therefore, **"students each have"** is correct.

---

### **When to Use "Each Has"**

- Use **"has"** when the sentence starts with **"each"** as the subject:
    - _Each of the students has a book._
    - _Each student has received a reward._

---

### **When to Use "Each Have"**

- Use **"have"** when the subject is plural, and **"each"** follows it:
    - _The players each have a different jersey._
    - _The students each have their own desk._

## 🧠 Step
This line is NOT about logic — it’s about **geometry**.
Think of the pairs in a matrix:

| f1 \ f2 | 1   | 2   | 3   | 4   |
| ------- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **1**   | A,A | A,B | A,C | A,D |
| **2**   | B,A | B,B | B,C | B,D |
| **3**   | C,A | C,B | C,C | C,D |
| **4**   | D,A | D,B | D,C | D,D |
### `f1.length = f2.length`
This says:
> “Only keep pairs where the lengths are equal.”
So Postgres **throws away most pairs**:

(A,A) (A,B) (A,D)
(B,A) (B,B) (B,D)
(C,C)
(D,A) (D,B) (D,D)

9.  **Navigation Functions: LAG() and LEAD()**

```sql
SELECT 
  customer_id, 
  payment_date, 
  amount,
  LAG(amount) OVER(PARTITION BY customer_id ORDER BY payment_date) as last_payment_amount
FROM payment;
```

**The Result:**

| customer_id | payment_date | amount | last_payment_amount |
| :---------- | :----------- | :----- | :------------------ |
| 1           | Jan 1        | 10.00  | NULL                |
| 1           | Jan 15       | 12.00  |  **10.00**          |
| 1           | Feb 1        | 8.00   |  **12.00**          |
